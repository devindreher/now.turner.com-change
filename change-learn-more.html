Phases & Reactions to Change

For most people, change can be unsettling — even if it's positive change. It can help to understand the different phases of change and what feelings are common during each phase. Being able to name what you're going through and how you feel is the first step to creating your new reality.

 
Change is an event that is situational and external to us. Something stops and something new begins. It's typically an outcome or results-focused and has a stop/start quality. For example, having a baby, getting a new job, or the launch of a project.

A transition is our internal psychological reorientation to change. It's how we come to terms with the new reality and adapt to the new situation. Until we successfully transition from an old way to a new way, the overall change won't be successful. We go through transitions at our own pace.
 
 

We transition through change in three basic phases: the ending, the neutral zone, and a new beginning.

During the the ending, we let go of our old ways of doing things.

The neutral zone is the heart of the transition process. You aren't doing what you used to do, but you may not be doing things in a new way, yet. For some, this is a time of confusion while for others it's a time of curiosity and receptiveness.

The new beginning is when we begin to feel "ourselves" again. We start to understand the new reality and identify with the situation.

Adapted from William Bridges and Linkage, 1998 & 2005.

As we transition through each phase, we experience different emotions and reactions. These are neither "good" or "bad." Help for People Leaders

Imagine a person riding an elephant. The rider is able to see the path ahead and make adjustments accordingly. The elephant is powerful and driven by emotion and instinct — if it doesn't want to move forward, there are few things the rider can do to force it to continue the journey.

The rider in the above scenario represents the logical part of our brain while the elephant is the emotional part. The latter is bigger and can overpower our logic. As a leader, you can "hack" the brain of your team by directing the rider (clarifying), motivating (uncovering the needs/desires of the team), and shaping the path (eliminating as many barriers as possible).

Build Resiliency

Resilience is not something you are born with or without. It's a set of behaviors, thoughts, and actions that can be learned and developed. Being resilient doesn't mean you don't experience challenges or setbacks, but rather you simply "bounce back" and achieve despite challenges and setbacks.
