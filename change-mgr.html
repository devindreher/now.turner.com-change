Change Information for Leaders

The tempo of change continues to speed up. In some cases it feels downright dizzying and the demands of being a leader make striving for success in turbulent times a challenge. The methods, tools, and tips contained here will support you as you help teams adapt, accept, and commit to change.

Pulling Your Team Together

For an organizational change to be successful, leaders must demonstrate strong, decisive commitment to the project. Strong commitment is vital so that the change agents and employees are effective. Pulling your team together to share information and uncover issues is an effective way in demonstrating your strong commitment.


1. Developing a Communications Plan

Sooner or later, you'll have to talk to your team about the change. Answering these 4P questions will help you craft your communications that'll move your team from complying with the change to being committed.


Purpose.

  • Why are we doing this?
  • What would happen if the organization didn't change?

Picture.

  • What will "the other side" look like?
  • What's changing and what's not?
  • How will Turner be more effective as a result?

Plan.

  • What's going to happen?
  • What'll happen 1st? 2nd? 3rd? ...

Part.

  • What's my role?
  • How will I be impacted?
  • What role can my team play in making this successful?

2. Preparing Your Responses.

If you anticipate specific concerns and deal with them early, then you can avoid unnecessary resistance. Think about what you can say about the benefits/realities of the change. Identify anticipated challenges and create messages that will address each. Don't forget to consider how you'll deliver them (e.g., email, face-to-face, phone, etc.)

3. Talking With and Listening to Your Team.

Here are some ideas for when it comes time to connect with your team:


  • Hold an initial meeting or conference call with each of your direct reports.
  • Hold one-on-one conversations with each direct report.
  • Commit to 10 minutes each day to talk with your team about the transformation.
  • Hold an off-site meeting with your direct reports.


Keep in mind...

  • Engage in open and frequent dialog.
  • Own the effectiveness of the transformation with your direct reports.
  • Get positive "opinion leaders" involved with specific tranformation actions within your department.
  • Meet regularly with your direct reports — individually and collectively — to understand issues and progress, and to provide feedback for issues.
  • Repeat key messages.
  • Don't be afraid to say "I don't know," but follow-up with when you will know.
  • Pay attention to body language and probe further to surface concerns and other issues.
  • Build an environment for constructive dissent (it's OK to raise problems in a constructive manner).
  • Remember that resistance is normal — don't discount people's losses, nor belittle or rush over their feelings.

4. Checking-In & Propelling Success

Here are some ideas for monitoring progress:


  • Pay attention to how people respond to the change activities.
  • Ensure that positive efforts are recognized and rewarded.

Keep in mind...

  • Recognize that people who make early efforts and are positive and constructive, even if their results fall short.
  • Recognize and reward the people who reinforce the culture while achieving results.
  • Make it your job to hunt down and remove barriers to the transformation.
  • Confront fellow managers and leaders who undercut the change.
  • Be paitent.

Motivating Your Team

In most change situations, we typically focus on strategy, structure, culture, or systems. This leads us to miss something critical: for anything to change people have to start behaving differently — all changes are behavior changes at the core. These behavior changes happen in highly successful situations mostly by speaking to people's feelings. We need to find ways to motivate others to see the problems or solutions in ways that influence emotions, not just thought.

Here are 9 ways to motivate others to change:

  • Recognition
  • Empathy
  • Being a part of a bigger mission
  • Winning
  • Autonomy & trust
  • Competition with industry peers
  • Inspiration
  • Discontentment
  • Instrinsic

If you need clarification around any of these, feel free to reach out to Turner People Development. They can help further define what motivates your team.

 

Assessing Personal Impacts On Your Team

Why do we care about the personal impact on our team?

A person's reaction to change is very personal. Every transition begins with an ending — a loss — even when the change is positive.

We must first acknowledge the loss, whether it's real or perceived, permanent or temporary, tangible or intangible. The loss can affect the depth of the transition and the time it takes to get to the new beginning.

Also, don't forget about yourself! Acknowledge your loss, too, so you can effectively begin to transition.

 

First, identify what your loss is and then ask your team members. Try to view the impact from their perspective. Ask yourself: who stands to lose something related to this change and what do they stand to lose?
