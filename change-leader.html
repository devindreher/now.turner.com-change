How We React to Change

Change vs. Transition

A transition is internal — it's what happens in your minds as we go through changes. Ironically, transitions begin with an ending (losing or letting go), then shift into a neutral zone where we might feel confused or disconnected. The last stage of a transition is the new beginning where we take hold and grow familiar with our new realities.



Signs of a Transition

You'll often see an increase in these 5 signs during the ending or neutral zones. Of course, there's an acronym to remember them: GRASS.


  1. Guilt.

    Change creates winners and losers. Winners can feel guilty for benefitting from a change.

  2. Resentment.

    Starting and recycling malicious behaviors, passive-aggressive behaviors, or sabotage.

  3. Anxiety.

    People get anxious. That anxiety can linger until post-change. They may grow edgy and may overreact to events or occurrences.

  4. Self-Absorption.

    Transitions turn a person's focus back on his/herself and undermines the teamwork. Office doors get closed and people start working on their resumes.

  5. Stress.

    Teams may experience increased absenteeism, worker compensation claims, and use of employee assistance programs.

 

Being a Change Sponsor

For an organizational change to be successful, sponsors must demonstrate strong, decisive commitment to the project. Strong commitment is vital so that the agents and targets are effective.


1. Developing a Communications Plan

Sooner or later, you'll have to talk to your team about the change. Answering these 4P questions will help you craft your communications that'll move your team from complying with the change to being committed.


Purpose.

  • Why are we doing this?
  • What would happen if the organization didn't change?

Picture.

  • What will "the other side" look like?
  • What's changing and what's not?
  • How will Turner be more effective as a result?

Plan.

  • What's going to happen?
  • What'll happen 1st? 2nd? 3rd? ...

Part.

  • What's my role?
  • How will I be impacted?
  • What role can my team play in making this successful?

2. Preparing Your Responses.

If you anticipate specific concerns and deal with them early, then you can avoid unnecessary resistance. Thinka bout what you can say about the benefits/realities of the change. Identify anticipated challenges and create messages that will address each. Don't forget to consider how you'll deliver them (e.g., email, face-to-face, phone, etc.)

3. Talking With and Listening to Your Team.

Here are some ideas for when it comes time to connect with your team:


  • Hold an initial meeting or conference call with each of your direct reports.
  • Hold one-on-one conversations with each direct report.
  • Commit to 10 minutes each day to talk with your team about the transformation.
  • Hold an off-site meeting with your direct reports.


Keep in mind...

  • Engage in open and frequent dialog.
  • Own the effectiveness of the transformation with your direct reports.
  • Get positive "opinion leaders" involved with specific tranformation actions within your department.
  • Meet regularly with your direct reports — individually and collectively — to understand issues and progress, and to provide feedback for issues.
  • Repeat key messages.
  • Don't be afraid to say "I don't know," but follow-up with when you will know.
  • Pay attention to body language and probe further to surface concerns and other issues.
  • Build an environment for constructive dissent (it's OK to raise problems in a constructive manner).
  • Remember that resistance is normal — don't discount people's losses, nor belittle or rush over their feelings.

4. Checking-In & Propelling Success

Here are some ideas for monitoring progress:


  • Pay attention to how people respond to the change activities.
  • Ensure that positive efforts are recognized and rewarded.

Keep in mind...

  • Recognize that people who make early efforts and are positive and constructive, even if they're results fall short.
  • Recognize and reward the people who reinforce the culture while achieving results.
  • Make it your job to hunt down and remove barriers to the transformation.
  • Confront fellow managers and leaders who undercut the change.
  • Be paitent.

Identifying resistance.

  • Engage in two-way communications with the employees impacted by the change. This can include Q&A sessions, interviews, brainstorming sessions, group meetings, and one-on-one encounters to identify sensitivities and resistance.
  • Interview managers responsible for past changes. Collect data to assist in determining the groups and locations where resistance has been historically evident.
  • Gauge reactions, watch body language, and pay attention to casual remarks. Resistance may exist in a subtle, but consequential way.
  • Set deliverables and monitor their progress. Missed deadlines, missed meetings, absenteeism, illness, lack of cooperation, indifference, and poor performance may be symptoms of resistance.
 

Coaching Through Change

If you have someone who's struggling with the change, reflect on what you've noticed then answer the questions below:


  1. What behaviors is the person demonstrating right now? Specifically, what is this individual saying and doing related to the changes?
  2. Based on these behaviors, what do you believe may be the underlying cause of their fear or resistance?
  3. What stage of the transition (i.e., ending, neutral, new beginning) do you think this person may be in? What signs of transition do you see?
  4. What actions would be appropriate for you to take in order to help this person accept and participate in the change?
